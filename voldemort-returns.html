He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named Has Returned: The Truth the Ministry of Magic Refuses to Acknowledge

Three eyewitnesses. The sworn testimony of Albus Dumbledore. A dead Hogwarts student. A Triwizard Cup turned Portkey. And a Ministry still insisting the Dark Lord's return is, in the words of Minister Fudge, "the hysterical fantasy of a troubled boy." The Daily Prophet has examined every piece of available evidence. Our conclusion is not comfortable reading.
By Rita Skeeter, Senior Correspondent · Barnabas Cuffe, Editor · Special Investigation

There is a name that wizarding Britain has spent fourteen years learning not to say. It has become habit, custom, superstition — call it what you will. We say He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named, or You-Know-Who, or simply nothing at all, and in that silence we have perhaps been too comfortable. Too willing, in our collective relief at the events of October 1981, to assume that the thing which ended in Godric's Hollow stayed ended.

This newspaper will, in this report, use the name that fear has kept from our columns for a generation. Lord Voldemort has returned. We say this not to cause panic — though we suspect that panic, in measured doses, may be precisely what the wizarding public requires right now. We say it because our investigation of the events of the twenty-fourth of June, 1995, at the conclusion of the Triwizard Tournament's final task, has left us with no intellectually honest alternative.

On that evening, two Hogwarts champions entered the Tournament maze to compete for the Triwizard Cup. One came back. The other — Cedric Diggory, seventeen years old, Hufflepuff prefect, the son of Amos Diggory of the Department for the Regulation and Control of Magical Creatures — was returned to the Hogwarts grounds draped over Harry Potter's arms, dead. The Cup that was supposed to represent his triumph had, according to Potter, been transformed into a Portkey that transported both champions to a graveyard in Little Hangleton. And in that graveyard, Potter says, he watched Lord Voldemort rise again.

Minister Cornelius Fudge, in statements issued in the immediate aftermath of the Tournament's conclusion, has described Potter's account as "the product of extreme trauma" and has questioned, with what this newspaper considers breathtaking insensitivity, whether the boy's "well-documented history of unusual attention-seeking behaviour" should be taken into account when assessing his reliability as a witness. He has not, it is worth noting, offered any alternative explanation for how Cedric Diggory died.

"Minister Fudge has not offered any alternative explanation for how Cedric Diggory died. Until he does, his dismissal of Harry Potter's testimony deserves to be treated with precisely the level of confidence it merits: none."

— Editorial position, The Daily Prophet · 25 June 1995
The Evidence — What We Know Happened at Little Hangleton

Potter's account of the events in the Little Hangleton graveyard has been provided to this newspaper in a sworn, witnessed statement, co-signed by Albus Dumbledore himself. We summarise it here, having verified as many of the independently confirmable elements as our access permits.

Upon arriving in the graveyard via the Portkey, Potter and Diggory were confronted by the figure of Peter Pettigrew — the man long believed dead at the hands of Sirius Black, whose conviction for Pettigrew's murder we note now sits in rather different light — carrying what Potter describes as "a creature, barely formed, wrapped in robes." Pettigrew struck Diggory with the killing curse before Potter could react. He then bound Potter to a grave marker — identified by Potter, and subsequently confirmed by Ministry maps, as the grave of Tom Riddle Senior, the Muggle father of Lord Voldemort.

What followed — and here we are reporting Potter's account, which we note is corroborated in its broad strokes by Dumbledore's own testimony and by the physical evidence examined by Hogwarts staff — was a ritual. Bone from the father's grave. Blood taken from Potter's arm against his will. And flesh willingly surrendered by Pettigrew himself. From these ingredients, a body was apparently restored. And from that body, Voldemort spoke.

He then, according to Potter, summoned his Death Eaters — those former followers who had evaded Azkaban by claiming Imperius curse compulsion — to witness his return. He named those he identified as present. Among the names Potter has provided to us are several that will surprise our readers considerably. We are withholding those names pending independent verification, because we are, despite what our critics occasionally suggest, a newspaper and not a pamphlet.

The Ministry Response — Denial as Policy

Cornelius Fudge arrived at Hogwarts in the immediate aftermath of the night's events. By all accounts, including those of several Hogwarts staff members willing to speak candidly, Dumbledore presented the Minister with Potter's testimony, with his own corroborating account, and with the physical evidence — Potter's wound, the restored Portkey, the testimony of the other champions regarding what they witnessed at the maze entrance. Fudge's response, described by one senior Hogwarts figure as "frankly cowardly," was to leave.

In the days since, the Ministry has issued three separate statements, each one more insistent than the last that Voldemort has not returned, that Harry Potter is not a reliable witness, and that Albus Dumbledore — who this newspaper notes holds an Order of Merlin First Class, has served as Supreme Mugwump of the International Confederation of Wizards, and has spent the better part of sixty years as the foremost authority on the Dark Arts in Britain — is engaged in a campaign of deliberate scaremongering for reasons the Ministry has not yet troubled itself to specify.

This, frankly, is not a credible position. It is a political position. There is a difference, and in this instance the difference is potentially catastrophic.

⚰ In Memoriam — Cedric Diggory
Cedric Amos Diggory, 17, of Ottery St Catchpole, Devon. Hufflepuff, Hogwarts Class of 1995. Triwizard Champion. Quidditch Captain, Hufflepuff team, 1993–95. Son of Amos and Meredith Diggory.

Cedric Diggory died on the night of 24 June 1995, under circumstances that the Ministry of Magic has so far declined to investigate with any seriousness. He was, by all accounts, a young man of exceptional character and considerable talent. He deserved, at the very minimum, to have his death taken seriously.

The Daily Prophet extends its sincere condolences to Amos and Meredith Diggory, and calls upon the Ministry of Magic to open a full inquiry into the circumstances of their son's death.
Why Fudge Is Wrong — And Why It Matters

The Ministry's position is, on one level, understandable. The return of Lord Voldemort would represent the most significant threat to wizarding Britain since his original rise to power in the 1970s. Acknowledging that threat would require action — resources, mobilisation, the dismantling of comfortable arrangements. It would require admitting that the fourteen years since his apparent defeat at Godric's Hollow have been not a genuine era of peace but a reprieve. It would require confronting the fact that during those fourteen years, many of those who served Voldemort most eagerly were never properly held to account.

None of this is comfortable. None of it is politically convenient. We understand why the Minister prefers the alternative. We simply do not think the alternative is available to him any longer.

Voldemort's first reign lasted eleven years. It claimed the lives of hundreds of witches and wizards — of Muggle-borns systematically targeted for their heritage, of those who opposed him, of those who simply had the misfortune of being in his way. The reason it ended when it did had nothing to do with Ministry intervention and everything to do with a one-year-old child in a cottage in Godric's Hollow. The Ministry has been dining on that borrowed peace ever since.

We are not dining any longer. A boy is dead. A graveyard in Little Hangleton has been identified by Aurors — who visited it in an unofficial capacity, three of them on their own initiative — as showing signs of recent magical activity of a kind and scale inconsistent with ordinary use. Harry Potter has marks on his arm that are consistent with a blade wound. And Dumbledore has staked his considerable reputation on the truth of what that boy reported seeing.

We do not know what happens next. We do not know how long the Ministry's denial can be sustained before events make it untenable. What we know is that the wizarding world is, once again, facing a threat it is not currently being equipped to face. And that the newspaper whose masthead has graced Diagon Alley for two hundred and fifty years has an obligation to say so, regardless of how comfortable it is for those in power to hear it.

Lord Voldemort has returned. The Ministry of Magic knows it. And every witch and wizard in Britain deserves to know it too.
