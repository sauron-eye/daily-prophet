He Has Gone: Mass Murderer Sirius Black Vanishes From Azkaban in the Fortress's First-Ever Escape

Black, serving twelve life sentences for the murders of Peter Pettigrew and thirteen Muggles, and for the betrayal of the Potters to He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named, was discovered absent from his cell in the early hours of Sunday morning. Minister for Magic Cornelius Fudge has appealed for calm. The nation is anything but calm.
By Rita Skeeter, Senior Correspondent & Barnabas Cuffe, Editor-in-Chief · Azkaban Fortress, North Sea

The walls of Azkaban have never been breached. That was the incontrovertible truth upon which the entire apparatus of wizarding law and order has rested for three centuries. Prisoners go in. They do not come out — not alive, and not of their own volition. Until, that is, the early hours of Sunday the first of August, 1993, when Sirius Black, the most reviled prisoner in the fortress's history, simply ceased to be there.

This newspaper can confirm exclusively that Black — convicted in November 1981 of the cold-blooded murder of twelve Muggles in a London street, the killing of fellow Hogwarts alumnus and Ministry worker Peter Pettigrew, and the treacherous betrayal of James and Lily Potter to He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named — was discovered missing from his cell during a routine morning check at approximately five o'clock. The Dementor assigned to his corridor reportedly hovered outside an empty cell for some minutes before raising what passed, among those creatures, for an alarm.

Minister for Magic Cornelius Fudge arrived at the fortress by Portkey shortly before eight o'clock. He was seen speaking at length with the fortress commander and with senior members of the Dementor guard before emerging to address a small gathering of reporters — this correspondent among them — on the frost-bitten jetty. His face, ordinarily the picture of ruddy confidence, was the colour of old parchment.

"I want to reassure the wizarding public," he said, his voice rather less steady than his words, "that every available resource of the Ministry is being deployed. The Auror Office has been placed on its highest alert. We will find him." He then declined to take questions and returned inside. It was not, by any measure, a reassuring performance.

How Did He Do It? The Question No One Can Answer

The mechanics of the escape remain, at time of press, entirely unknown. What is known — and what makes this event so profoundly disturbing — is the environment from which Black managed to vanish. Azkaban is not merely a prison. It is an island fortress in the most remote and storm-battered stretch of the North Sea, reachable only by broom or by Portkey under Ministry authorisation. It is guarded not by human wardens but by Dementors, the foulest creatures known to the wizarding world, beings for whom the sustaining of hope, will, and rational thought in their prisoners is not a side effect but an active and perpetual project of destruction.

No prisoner has ever, in the fortress's three-hundred-year history, broken free. Several have tried. Most were found, within days, wandering the North Sea coast in states of such advanced mental deterioration that they were transferred directly to the permanent ward at St Mungo's Hospital for Magical Maladies. The rest were returned to their cells. Azkaban, it had always been said, was impossible to escape from because even the desire to escape required a state of mind that Azkaban systematically destroyed.

And yet Black appears to have retained precisely that. The question is how.

Prison governor Araminta Blishen, speaking reluctantly and on strict condition of anonymity, told this reporter that Black's cell had been "unchanged, undamaged, and unremarkable in every respect." The door had not been forced. No tunnel had been excavated. No magical trace — no residue of Apparition, Portkey creation, or transfiguration — had been detected. The cell was simply empty, the thin blanket folded with, she said, "an almost contemptuous neatness."

"The cell was simply empty. The blanket had been folded with an almost contemptuous neatness. In thirty years of service, I have never seen anything like it."

— Anonymous Prison Official, Azkaban Fortress · Quoted to this newspaper
Black's History — The Man Behind the Mugshot

Sirius Black was born in 1959 into one of the most ancient and prominent pure-blood families in Britain, the Blacks of Grimmauld Place. He attended Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry from 1971, where he was Sorted into Gryffindor — a placement that shocked his family, who had, for centuries, produced Slytherins of note. He was understood to have been close friends with James Potter, the late father of the celebrated Boy Who Lived, as well as with the late Remus Lupin and, fatally, Peter Pettigrew.

Upon leaving Hogwarts, Black was understood to have joined the Order of the Phoenix, the organisation led by Albus Dumbledore that opposed He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named during the darkest period of the last war. It was precisely this proximity to the resistance movement — and to the Potters specifically — that made his eventual treachery so devastating.

The Potters, in the months before their murder in October 1981, had placed their faith in a Fidelius Charm, a sophisticated enchantment that conceals a secret within a single living soul — the Secret-Keeper. Black, as the couple's most trusted friend, was named their Secret-Keeper. What followed was, Ministry officials have consistently maintained, a calculated act of deliberate betrayal: Black revealed the Potters' whereabouts in Godric's Hollow to He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named. James and Lily Potter died that night. Only their infant son, Harry, survived — in circumstances that remain the subject of speculation and considerable wonder.

When Aurors arrived at the street in Muggle London the following morning, they found Black standing amid a crater twelve feet wide, laughing. Pettigrew, who had confronted Black after deducing his treachery, was represented by nothing more than a bloodied finger. Of the twelve Muggles killed by the explosion Black is alleged to have caused, nothing identifiable remained. Black offered no defence at trial. He was sentenced, by unanimous Wizengamot vote, to life imprisonment in Azkaban. He was twenty-two years old.

The Public Reaction — Fear Spreading Fast

Across Wizarding Britain, the reaction to news of the escape has been swift, visceral, and uniform: terror. Flourish and Blotts reported a run on copies of Confronting the Faceless and Dark Creature Defence for the Amateur at ten o'clock this morning. The Leaky Cauldron was, by eleven, operating with all protective enchantments engaged. Tom the barman told this reporter that three families had cancelled long-standing room bookings "just to be safe."

Madam Rosmerta of the Three Broomsticks in Hogsmeade — a village that sits in uncomfortably close proximity to Hogwarts School, where a certain Harry Potter will shortly be returning for his third year — was blunter still. "I've been saying for years that relying on Dementors was asking for trouble," she said, slamming a Butterbeer onto the counter with unnecessary force. "They're not guards. They're not reliable. They're barely controllable. And now look."

The wider implication — the one that no Ministry spokesperson has yet been willing to address directly but that every witch and wizard in Britain is thinking — is obvious: Sirius Black, who served He-Who-Must-Not-Be-Named, is free. And Harry Potter, the boy for whose family Black was directly responsible, returns to Hogwarts in September.

⚠ Ministry Security Guidance — Issued This Morning
The Auror Office urges all witches and wizards to observe the following precautions until further notice:

DO NOT approach or attempt to detain Sirius Black. He is considered highly dangerous and is believed to be practised in the Dark Arts.

DO report any sighting immediately via the Floo Network emergency channel (Auror Office Direct) or by owl marked URGENT.

DO NOT use Muggle communication channels (telephone, post). Ministry Obliviators are already working to contain Muggle awareness of the escape.

A reward of 10,000 Galleons is offered for information directly leading to Black's recapture. Informants will be protected by Ministry charter.
The Dementor Question — Can They Be Trusted?

Perhaps the most unsettling aspect of this morning's news is not Black's escape itself — monstrous and unprecedented as it is — but what it implies about the Dementors entrusted with his containment. Those hundred and more creatures, whose presence at Azkaban has been the central pillar of wizarding penal policy for three centuries, have failed at the one task they were placed there to perform.

Senior Azkaban official Belinda Cresswell-Booth, reached by owl early this morning, was defensive but visibly troubled. "The Dementors are not infallible," she said, choosing her words with evident care. "They are — there is simply no more diplomatic way to say this — deeply affected by what has occurred. They cannot explain it. They were present. They performed their rounds. And the prisoner was gone." She paused. "What I can tell you is that they are very — they are not at all pleased."

Displeased Dementors, for anyone who has not had the misfortune of encountering the creatures in their normal state, represents a prospect of truly exceptional grimness. The Ministry would do well to remember that creatures employed because they are beyond mercy are also, necessarily, beyond management.

Albus Dumbledore, Headmaster of Hogwarts and a man whose opinion on matters of dark consequence is generally worth soliciting, had not responded to this newspaper's requests for comment at time of press. Those who know him suggest that his silence, in this instance, is itself a kind of answer.
