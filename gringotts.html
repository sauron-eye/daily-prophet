Unprecedented Break-In at Gringotts Wizarding Bank — High-Security Vault 713 Targeted on the Busiest Day of the Year

For the first time in Gringotts' nine-hundred-year history, an unknown intruder penetrated the bank's deepest vaults. Goblin officials insist nothing of value was taken. Ministry sources are not so sure. Suspicion mounts over the timing — the very same morning Harry Potter made his first visit to the bank.
By Imelda Grout, Financial Correspondent & Reginald Moorhead, Crime Editor · Diagon Alley, London

Contents of Vault — Classified by Gringotts

There is a saying among the goblins of Gringotts, engraved in the white marble above the great burnished bronze doors of the bank's entrance on Diagon Alley, that has stood as both advertisement and warning for nearly a millennium: rob us if you dare. It is not, the goblins have always made perfectly clear, an invitation. It is a challenge they have, until yesterday, never had cause to regret issuing.

That record ended on the first day of August 1991. At some point between the opening of the bank at nine o'clock in the morning and the routine security sweep conducted at midnight, an unknown individual or individuals entered Vault 713 — one of Gringotts' most heavily guarded private vaults, located at a depth of several hundred feet below Diagon Alley — and removed whatever it was that vault contained. Goblin officials confirmed the breach in a tersely worded statement issued at six o'clock this morning, while insisting, with rather more emphasis than seemed wholly natural, that "nothing of consequence" had been taken.

This newspaper does not find that reassuring. Vault 713 is not, by any definition, a casual storage facility. It sits among the most security-intensive in the bank's subterranean network — the sort of vault that requires a specific Gringotts key, goblin escort, and cart journey of considerable duration and intestinal fortitude to access. The sort of vault, in other words, that people use when they have something they very badly do not want anyone else to have.

The question of what that something was — and who took it, and why — is one that Gringotts, the Ministry of Magic, and an increasingly anxious wizarding public are now confronting with quite different degrees of candour.

The Scene on Diagon Alley — First of August, 1991

The first of August is, under ordinary circumstances, the most frenetic day of the year on Diagon Alley. Hogwarts letters having arrived the previous week, families from across wizarding Britain converge on the alley to purchase school supplies, exchange currency, and conduct a year's worth of financial business before the September term begins. Gringotts, on such days, operates at something approaching organised chaos.

It was into exactly this environment that, according to multiple witnesses, Rubeus Hagrid — Keeper of Keys and Grounds at Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry — arrived at the bank accompanied by a young boy later identified as Harry Potter, the newly discovered heir to his late parents' Gringotts account. This newspaper is not suggesting that either Hagrid or young Potter had any involvement in what subsequently occurred. We are, however, noting that Hagrid visited Vault 713 during that same visit. Gringotts declined to comment on whether Hagrid had authorisation to access the vault, or on behalf of whom he might have been acting. The Ministry also declined to comment, which is, as our readers will know, frequently the most illuminating response of all.

Several witnesses near the cart terminus reported seeing Hagrid emerge from the lower vault levels cradling what one described as "a very small parcel" wrapped in plain brown paper. Hagrid was not available for comment. Albus Dumbledore was not available for comment. The Ministry was emphatically not available for comment.

"Nothing of consequence was taken." Those four words are doing a great deal of work in Gringotts' statement this morning. This newspaper will be asking what, precisely, they are being asked to carry.

— Editorial, The Daily Prophet · 2 August 1991
How Gringotts Security Works — And How It Failed

To understand why yesterday's breach is so extraordinary, it is worth understanding what Gringotts security actually consists of. The bank's vaults are protected by a layered system of enchantments that have been refined and extended over nine centuries of goblin craftsmanship. The outer vaults — those accessible to ordinary account holders — are protected by standard locking charms, Thief's Downfall enchantments, and goblin-operated cart transport that serves as the primary access mechanism.

The deeper vaults, of which 713 is among the most fortified, employ additional measures that goblin officials have historically declined to detail publicly. What is known from precedent and from the accounts of past staff is that these measures include fire-breathing dragon guards — creatures of the Ukrainian Ironbelly and Norwegian Ridgeback varieties have been reported in the lowest levels — proximity alarms keyed to specific goblin magic, and enchantments designed to multiply the weight of any object removed from the vault without proper authorisation. The combination, Gringotts has always maintained, is impenetrable to any being, magical or otherwise.

Yesterday, something penetrated it. Gringotts' head of security, a particularly formidable goblin who goes by the name Ragnok, declined to make any public statement beyond confirming the breach had occurred. Three of his junior colleagues, speaking to this reporter under condition of strict anonymity, described an atmosphere within the bank this morning that they variously characterised as "extremely serious," "genuinely frightening," and, from the most senior of the three, simply: "unprecedented."

That word — unprecedented — seems to follow wizarding Britain's most alarming events like a shadow these days. We heard it on the morning the Dark Lord fell in Godric's Hollow. We heard it when the first Muggle-Borns began disappearing in 1971. Now we hear it beneath the streets of Diagon Alley, in a bank whose nine-century security record ended some time between the hours of nine yesterday morning and midnight.

The Vault 713 Question — What Was Inside?

This is the question that Gringotts, the Ministry, and a growing number of senior wizarding officials are declining, with remarkable unanimity, to answer. Vault 713 is registered, according to financial records held at the Ministry of Magic — records which this newspaper has obtained through a source we are not at liberty to name — to a private Hogwarts account. Beyond that, the trail goes cold. The vault contents are, as with all Gringotts holdings, protected by strict goblin confidentiality provisions that predate the founding of the Ministry itself.

What we can say — and this we report with appropriate caution, drawing on background conversations with four individuals who were either present at the bank yesterday or have access to Ministry intelligence — is that the object removed from Vault 713 was small enough to be carried in a coat pocket, that it was placed there by a party acting on direct instructions from Albus Dumbledore, and that the break-in occurred on the same day that Hagrid removed something from that vault, several hours before the intruder is believed to have gained access.

The implication — that the vault was broken into to steal something that was, by that point, no longer there — is one our sources are unwilling to confirm or deny. It is, however, an implication that several of them allowed to sit without contradiction for a rather long time.

📜 Gringotts Bank — Key Facts
Founded: Circa 1100 · by Gringott, a goblin of considerable financial genius
Location: Diagon Alley, London (entrance via the Leaky Cauldron)
Ownership: Entirely goblin-operated; no Ministry oversight or shareholding
Security record: Zero successful thefts in 900+ years of operation (until yesterday)
Account confidentiality: Protected by goblin charter provisions older than the Ministry of Magic
Vault numbering: Higher numbers indicate vaults closer to surface level. Lower numbers — including 713 — are among the most secure and deepest vaults in the network.
Warning (engraved above entrance): "Enter, stranger, but take heed / Of what awaits the sin of greed."

Auror Rufus Scrimgeour, reached by this newspaper at the Ministry offices, confirmed that the Auror Office had been informed of the breach and was "cooperating fully with Gringotts' own investigation." He declined to speculate on who might have been responsible. He declined, more notably, to rule out that the breach was connected to any known dark wizard or organisation. He then said that he had to go and put the Floo back on.

It is worth dwelling, for a moment, on the timing. The first of August 1991 was a day of unusual significance in the wizarding world for reasons beyond the Hogwarts shopping rush. It was the day that Harry Potter — the boy who, at the age of fifteen months, somehow survived the killing curse directed at him by the most powerful dark wizard in history — first set foot in Diagon Alley. The day he first visited Gringotts. The day, sources suggest, that whatever was in Vault 713 was moved.

Whether this conjunction of events is coincidence, coordination, or something else entirely is a question this newspaper does not yet have the evidence to answer. It is, however, a question we intend to keep asking.
